## Common credit issues we help consumers understand

- Collection accounts: medical bills, utilities, credit cards, telecommunications accounts, apartments, personal loans, and more. Reviews examine original creditor, collection company, balance, dates, account ownership, and duplicate reporting. See [collections before mortgage approval](https://superiorcreditrepaironline.com/collections-before-mortgage-approval/).
- Late payments: compare reported late payments with statements, bank records, payment confirmations, hardship agreements, and creditor correspondence. See [late payments before mortgage approval](https://superiorcreditrepaironline.com/late-payments-before-mortgage-approval/).
- High credit‑card utilization: verify reported limits, statement balances, and payment dates before making major changes. See [high utilization before mortgage approval](https://superiorcreditrepaironline.com/high-credit-utilization-before-mortgage-approval/).
- Charge‑offs: may still show balances or appear alongside collection accounts; review dates, balances, and creditor names carefully.
- Repossessions: may involve deficiency balances after a sale; compare original account and related collection activity for duplication or inconsistencies.
- Medical collections: preserve bills, explanations of benefits, insurance correspondence, and payment confirmations for accurate review.
- Identity and mixed‑file errors: incorrect names, addresses, employers, or unfamiliar accounts may indicate mixed‑file or identity problems and require careful documentation.

## How the credit review process works

1. Gather current credit reports — save and review side by side; information can differ across bureaus.
2. Build an account inventory — list open/closed accounts, collections, charge‑offs, inquiries, repossessions, and unfamiliar entries with balances, limits, dates, and reporting companies.
3. Separate possible errors from accurate information — identify duplicates, wrong dates, or information belonging to another person; accurate negative items may need a different strategy.
4. Gather supporting documents — statements, bank records, payment confirmations, settlement letters, insurance records, ID documents, and creditor correspondence.
5. Dispute inaccurate information when appropriate — be specific and factual; avoid blanket disputes or false statements.
6. Strengthen current habits — paying on time, reducing revolving balances, avoiding unnecessary applications, maintaining savings, and monitoring reports.

## Important questions to ask before mortgage preapproval

- Which credit reports and scoring models will the lender use?
- How are collections treated under the intended loan program?
- Should disputed accounts be resolved before underwriting?
- Would paying down revolving balances improve the application?
- Should the consumer avoid opening or closing accounts?
- How much cash should remain available for reserves and closing costs?
- Which documents may be required to explain older credit issues?
- Could a new vehicle loan or co‑signed account affect qualification?

A credit professional can help organize reports and documentation; the mortgage lender explains underwriting and loan‑program requirements.
